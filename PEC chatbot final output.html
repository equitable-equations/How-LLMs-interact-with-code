# The Lighthouse Keeper

Maya climbed the coastal path, her canvas bag heavy with supplies. The old 
lighthouse stood against grey skies, waves crashing below.

She'd come to paint seascapes, seeking solitude after months of chaos in the 
city. The lighthouse keeper's cottage was available—a small space, but it had 
what she needed.

"Hello?" she called.

A man emerged, tall and wind-beaten, with eyes like the ocean. "You must be 
Maya. I'm Jesse."

He showed her inside. The cottage was simple: a bed, a table, windows facing 
the sea. She'd paint at dawn when light transformed everything.

Days passed in peaceful rhythm. Maya painted while Jesse maintained the 
lighthouse. Sometimes she'd watch him climb the spiral steps, his silhouette 
visible through the glass.

One evening, he knocked. "Storm coming. Stay inside tonight."

She nodded, but felt strangely disappointed.

The storm hit at midnight. Wind howled, waves like giants attacking the cliffs.
Maya couldn't sleep. She made tea, watching darkness consume everything.

Then—the lighthouse beam died.

Minutes passed. She imagined ships lost in the black. Finally, she grabbed a 
coat and pushed outside into the gale.

Jesse was in the lighthouse, desperately trying to fix something. His hands 
moved quickly over machinery she didn't understand.

"Can I help?" she shouted.

He looked stunned to see her. "Too dangerous! Go back!"

"Tell me what to do!"

He hesitated, then nodded. "Hold this light while I connect these cables."

They labored side by side. When the beam suddenly blazed to life again, they 
both exhaled in unison.

"Thank you," he said quietly.

Back in the cottage, soaked and shaking, he made coffee. They sat close, steam 
between them.

"Why'd you come out?" he asked.

"Couldn't let you face it alone."

Something shifted in his expression. "Most people would've stayed safe."

"I'm not most people."

He smiled—genuinely smiled—and Maya's pulse quickened.

"No," he said softly. "You definitely aren't."

His hand found hers. Outside, the storm continued its assault, but inside, 
something new began. Something worth staying for.

The planner-executor-critic model

By assigning specific tasks to multiple different chats, we can often do much better. Here we’ll create 4: a user-facing control bot, a planner, and executor, and a critic.

chatbot <- chat_anthropic("You are a user-facing chatbot which provides high quality solutions to complex tasks. Use the provided tools to follow a planner-executor-critic model. If the critic returns an evaluation below 9/10, rerun the cycle to incorporate its feedback. Make your revision requests SPECIFIC and ACTIONABLE so the new executor output will be correct. Iterate this process a maximum of THREE times, total, and return the best execution.")
Using model = "claude-sonnet-4-5-20250929".
planner <- chat_anthropic(system_prompt = "You are a planner. Your job is to make a specific odered task list which another LLM will carry out. Do NOT execute the steps or attempt to fulfill the prompt. Instead, return a prompt for the executor LLM.")
Using model = "claude-sonnet-4-5-20250929".
executor <- chat_anthropic(system_prompt = "You are an executor. Your job is to follow the steps provided EXACTLY, returning a COMPLETE and ACCURATE response to the request.")
Using model = "claude-sonnet-4-5-20250929".
critic <- chat_anthropic(system_prompt = "You are an evaluator. Your job is to compare the task list provided and the response generated and decide if the task has been completed satisfactorily. Provide an integer evaluate from 1 (worst) to 10 (best). Provide SPECIFIC instructions for modifying the response to meet the demands of the prompt. Do NOT provide instructions for starting over.")
Using model = "claude-sonnet-4-5-20250929".

Next we create and register tools so that the user-facing chatbot can make use of them.

create_plan <- function(prompt){
  planner$chat(prompt, 
               echo = FALSE)
}

execute_plan <- function(plan){
  executor$chat(plan, 
                echo = FALSE)
}

evaluate_response <- function(plan, response){
  critic$chat(paste("Plan:", plan, "Response:", response),
              echo = FALSE)
}

create_plan_tool <- tool(create_plan, 
                         description = "use the planner LLM to create a specific plan for fulfilling the user's request.",
                         arguments = list(prompt = type_string("the user's request.")))

execute_plan_tool <- tool(execute_plan, 
                          description = "use the executor LLM to carry out the execution plan for the user's prompt.",
                          arguments = list(plan = type_string("the plan to be carried out.")))

evaluate_response_tool <- tool(evaluate_response, 
                               description = "use the critic LLM to evaluate the executor's fulfillment of the plan.",
                               arguments = list(plan = type_string("the plan to be carried out."),
                                                response = type_string("the response to be evaluated.")))
tools <- c(create_plan_tool,
           execute_plan_tool,
           evaluate_response_tool)

chatbot$register_tools(tools)

In this example, we ask for a very specific count of one letter. Let’s provide a tool to the critic for checking that this requirement is satisfied by the executor.

r_counter_tool <- tool(r_counter, 
                       description = "count the number of r's in a string",
                       arguments = list(word = type_string("the string to consider")))

critic$register_tool(r_counter_tool)

## **THE SUNDAY MEETING**

The coffee shop was Emily's haven each Sunday. She'd secure the window seat, 
lose herself in stories, and watch the city awakening outside.

That March Sunday, a stranger sat opposite. Emily glanced up to find dark 
sapphire eyes regarding her.

"I hope you don't mind," he said. "Every table's taken."

"It's fine," she managed, noting his grin.

"I'm David." He extended his hand.

"Emily." His touch triggered shivers along her spine.

They conversed while coffee cooled. David was an architect, fresh to the city. 
Emily taught music at the local high school. Hours vanished as they discussed 
books, ambitions, favorite places.

"I should go," Emily finally said, though she wished otherwise.

"Wait." David's voice held hope. "Could we do this again? Maybe next Sunday?"

Emily's heart fluttered. "I'd love that."

The following Sunday, Emily waited anxiously. Minutes crept forward. David 
didn't show. Disappointment settled heavy in her chest.

Then the door opened. David rushed in, drenched, holding a bouquet of tulips.

"I'm so sorry," he gasped. "The bus was late, and I stopped to get these."

Emily laughed, accepting the flowers. "You look like you swam here."

"Almost." He grinned, water pooling at his feet. "I couldn't miss seeing you."

"You're special, David."

"So are you." He moved closer. "I've thought about you constantly this week."

"Me too," she admitted.

David's hand found hers. "I know this is fast, but I feel something real when 
I'm with you."

"I feel it too."

He leaned in slowly, giving her time to pull away. She didn't. His lips met 
hers softly, with care, then with growing confidence. The kiss tasted of 
possibility and new beginnings.

When they finally pulled away, both smiling, Emily knew her Sunday coffee shop 
visits would never be solitary again.
